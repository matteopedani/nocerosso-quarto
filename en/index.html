BEST

Buoyancy Energy Storage Technology

A feasible solution for energy storage with buoys

Gravitational energy storage is an innovative method of storing energy, taking advantage of the force of gravity. This approach differs from traditional electrochemical batteries and represents a potentially revolutionary solution for managing the intermittency of renewable energy sources, such as solar and wind, which do not produce energy constantly.

Principle of operation

The system works in two phases: in the charging phase, the energy excess electricity from renewable sources is used for lift the dead bodies, which are blocks, to the surface of the sea of heavy material hanging from buoys. In the discharge phase, when there is one need for energy, the blocks are lowered to the seabed, exploiting gravity to produce kinetic energy, which then comes converted into electricity.

In our case the heavy object is a dead body the size of about 4 railway carriages full of stones hanging from a buoy of the same size which keeps it suspended.

Inside the buoy an electric motor lifts the dead body in moments of energy surplus or releases the dead body becoming an electric generator in moments of energy demand.

Everything is completed by a cable that holds the buoy to the seabed, and a marine power line connects the buoy with the motor-generator to the distribution network.

Advantages

The advantages of gravitational energy storage, or more specifically BEST, over other sources of energy include:

  • High efficiency: An overall efficiency higher than 80% has been estimated by independent studies.

  • Short activation time: The activation time in the order of 250/1000 milliseconds.

  • Accumulate energy without loss over time: The accumulators once positioned. They retain their energy without loss.

  • Intrahourly, daily or annual, multi-annual: they can both compensate energy requests with short and long cycles at the same cost and technology. In this regard it can replace at a lower cost the oil and gas storage, providing a competitive, industrial, financial and military advantage.

  • Possibility of using recycled materials: Fill the blocks with recycled aggregates, contributing to environmental sustainability.

  • Long useful life: Gravitational storage systems they have a long useful life and do not undergo degradation significant accumulation capacities over time, which is measured in decades.

  • Environmental integration: In places where there are accumulators, buoys physically inhibit fishing, realizing de facto marine protected areas, which allow reproduction of fish species.

  • Integration with fish farms: Close to the locations where accumulators are present, they can be made easily from fish farms.

  • Independence from climatic conditions: Unlike renewable sources such as solar and wind, which are dependent by weather conditions, gravitational storage is not influenced by the climate and can provide energy on demand.

  • Integration with renewables: This technology can be integrated with renewable energy sources to stabilize the electricity grid and manage the intermittency of production energy.

  • Low environmental impact: Unlike some sources renewables that can have a significant impact on the environment, such as the construction of large dams for energy hydroelectric, gravitational storage has an environmental impact Bass.

  • Low or no environmental risk: Unlike solutions alternatives such as offshore gas or oil storage, which can explode or leak. And even if they break, they don’t cause floods like dams.

  • Reduction of dependence on fossil fuels: Using excess energy from renewable sources to lift weights, it Gravitational storage helps reduce dependence on fossil fuels and improve energy security.

  • Military: storage, and the infrastructure that carries energy from the accumulators to the mains, it allows you to easily create automated refueling points for both naval units conventional, and for marine drones are of the underwater type (AUV – Autonomous Underwater Vehicle) or surface (USV – Unmanned Surface Vehicle)

    • Simple integration with hydrogen: They can be used in combination with electrolysers to generate hydrogen and oxygen as needed. Minimizing costs, risks and losses for hydrogen and oxygen storage.

  • They can be moved loaded, in complete safety, as long as they are positioned on a sufficiently deep seabed.

  • They have no polluting chemical emissions.

  • They don’t make any noise.

  • Even in shallow seas such as the North Sea, they can be useful to compensate for daily peaks. In fact, the dead bodies move a few centimeters per minute and can be used for hours before reaching the seabed even in the case of shallow waters such as in the North Sea.

  • They have a low or no visual impact, in fact the buoys can also be positioned slightly below the water surface if necessary.

  • They can support a port to energize it when ships are in port. Thus allowing large ships to be supported even with a distribution network insufficient to support the demand peaks created by large ships. All without generating the pollution associated with large ships.

Disadvantages

  • In places where accumulators are physically present inhibit trawling, and navigation must be regulated to take into account where they are positioned the buoys.

  • They have mobility limited by their mass.

  • If moved to shallow water, they lose their potential.

  • Some seas are too shallow to provide a large capacity for storage.

  • Even if the buoys themselves do not pollute the cable duct that connects them to the net affects the life of aquatic organisms in a significant way similar to other submarine cable pipelines

Comparison with other storage technologies

  • Hydroelectric Dams: Dams have been in use for longer a century both to generate energy and accumulate it through the pumping of water between basins at different altitudes. The dams hydroelectric plants are long-lived, some have exceeded a century activity. But dams have notable environmental impacts, either on the environment, both social. Also generating disasters like that of Vaiont, movements of entire villages as in China and Egypt, e.g not least the submergence of important artistic monuments. Without ignore the fact that they have practically already been created dams in economically profitable places, remaining to be exploited places more expensive in construction.

  • Electrochemical batteries: Lithium ion batteries, al lead, salt. They cost much more per unit of energy stored. And they have an estimated lifespan that hardly exceeds 5 - 10 years to be optimistic.

  • Hydrogen Hydrogen has storage and safety difficulties, it spreads from the walls of containers, and explodes easily. Making hydrogen interesting only for use in transportation.

Technologies involved

Generators, gearmotors, shipbuilding, iron buoys, ferrocement buoys, power lines, are all technologies that have survived more than 150 years of life and have reached maturity many years ago. This allows us to have a precise estimate of costs, and a precise forecast of the operational life of the “BEST”.

There are established and competitive global manufacturers for these components, allowing rapid industrial integration to be achieved.
